INTRODUCTION

To effectively mimic the assembly of nature’s biomachines, such as the Type 3 secretion system (T3SS) tip complex, requires some structural information about the system.

Therefore, our first task is to review and expand on the current knowledge of the individual proteins the tip complex is assembled from, as well as their stoichiometry and arrangement in complex.

We’ve distilled this information into a set of specifications we call the ‘Biological Blueprint’. This biological blueprint is the basis for the DNA designs that will scaffold the assembly of the T3SS needle tip complex.


Tip Monomer Structure: Understanding the Parts of Assembly

Domain Level Structure
The Crystal structures of several Needle Tip protein subunits have been solved including such as IpaD [1](Shigella flexneri), SipD [2](Salmonella typhii), and LcrV [3](Yersina pestis). Despite differences in their sequence, tip proteins share a common global structure composed 3 domains: a central coiled coil (green) and two globular domains (red and blue).

C- and N- Terminus Accessibility

The points of attachment between protein and DNA scaffold are at the termini of the protein sequence, so it is essential that these are accessible. In all three cases, both the N- and C- termini are located proximal to the native assembly scaffold the needle filament, and therefore ideal for localizing to a synthetic scaffold.

Stociometry of Needle Tip Complex

The Needle tip complex is generally thought to have pentameric radial symmetry as estimated by STEM [6], crosslinking [7], molecular modeling [1,8], and cryo electron microscopy [7]. It has been suggested that this may be a hetero-pentamer consisting of four needle tip proteins and one ‘translocon’ protein [1, 7]. However bacteria missing the gene for the ‘odd’ translocon protein still form Tip complexes [7], indicating a 5-fold symmetry is possible at the least.

Diameter of Native Scaffold

As we are designing an artificial scaffold for assembly, we looked at available models of the natural assembly scaffold for Shigella [4] and Salmonella [5].

Model of Shigella Needle Filament (PDB:2mme)

Model of Shigella Needle Filament (PDB:2lpz)

We used the distance function in pymol to measure a diameter range of 60-70Å. This is in agreement with scale of STEM [9] images and in CryoEM reconstructions of the Tip complex on the Filament [7,10].

Generating Molecular Models Based on Cryo-EM Density Maps

Finally we generated our own molecular models by docking the needle tip subunits into a calculated electron density map of the tip complex space.

In Chimera, EM density map of the needle filament (EMD:5352) alone was docked into a cryo-EM density map of the needle filament and tip complex(EMD:2801). Needle filament-only density data was then subtracted from filament and tip density data resulting in an electron density map of the needle tip filament alone. SipD crystal monomers (3nzz) were first manually docked into this tip density and the fitted using chimera ‘fit in map’ function.
